Beans

Beans come in many variations for many varied uses. We are primarily growing green-bean, also known as snap-bean, varieties, but there are also many great dry-beans out there. We found a little-known seed source of Appalachian Heirloom beans and can't wait to try out several of their highly acclaimed varieties in 2023.

  • Painted Pony
  • Rose
  • Logan Giant

Hidatsa Shield Figure

Snap or Dry Pole Bean

This pole bean was grown by the Hidatsa Indians of North Dakota. Although commercially available from several sources, we chose to purchase our seed stock from a small company located in North Dakota. They say that this variety is “Drought and heat tolerant, it is one of the most productive dry beans. … This beautiful, large, plump bean can be harvested throughout the growing season... providing you with green beans early in the season, followed by shellies (shelled out while the bean is formed but still soft), and, at the end of the season, as dry beans for long-term storage! This highly productive bean variety was named to the Slow Food USA's Ark of Taste for its wonderful flavor and cooking qualities. We use it in soups and stews, as it reheats nicely, without falling apart. Also makes a wonderful humus in place of chickpeas.” We are looking forward to growing this piece of American history!

Painted Pony

Snap or Dry Bush Bean

Seed Savers Exchange says of this variety is “A great dual purpose bean. Long thin pods are stringless and good for snap beans. The dry beans are excellent for soups and retain their markings when cooked. Very productive. Bush habit, 60 days for snaps, 80 days for dry beans.” We are looking forward to growing this multi-use bush bean!

Rose

Appalachian Cornfield Bean

This Kentucky Heirloom is a cornfield-type bean. Traditionally grown up cornstalks for support, these bean varieties can get quite tall and usually are more shade-tolerant. Most cornfield beans are better known as pole-beans today, since the practice of growing climbing beans up cornstalks has faded away with the Indians who first taught it to the settlers of yesteryear. Our seed source says Rose is “A very large blue speckled bean, this bean comes from the Rose Family in Panola, Madison County, Kentucky.” We are looking forward to growing a variety which is said to be very popular in parts of Appalachia.

Logan Giant

Appalachian Heirloom Bean

This is another heirloom from Appalachia. We don't know much about it yet, but we look forward to discovering the traits of Logan Giant and sharing them with you as soon as we are able. Our seed source says that Logan Giant is “very popular in much of West Virginia and other states as well, this brown-seeded bean is large and highly productive.”

Small Speckled Greasy Cut-Short

Appalachian Greasy Cut-Short Bean

A small bean with a long name! This cornfield-type bean's name deserves a little explaining for the uninitiated. Like many heirlooms, this bean is named for several of its characteristics. First, “Small Speckled”, because the pods don't grow extremely long and the mature seeds have a beautiful speckled pattern. Second, “Greasy”, because this variety doesn't have the fuzz found on most beans. Finally, “Cut-Short”, which references the trait of this variety to pack the seeds so closely in the pod that the ends of the seeds square off or are “cut short”. This is actually a highly desirable trait because there is more protein and less space in the pod as compared to average beans. We hope you'll give this great little bean with the long name a try! One last note, from our seed source: “From West Virginia, this bean is a small brown-speckled greasy cut-short with tightly packed seeds. Given to me by Lothar Baumann, whose brother obtained it while working in West Virginia, this bean is short but highly productive.”

Robert Hazelwood

Appalachian Half-Runner Bean

Similar in appearance and from a nearby locality to the Rose Cornfield Bean above, Robert Hazelwood only grows to a fraction of Rose's height (which makes picking those late-season beans a whole lot easier!). This is one of the few heirloom beans named after a man, as varieties usually sport a name like “Grandma Barnett” or “Aunt Nan's”. Our seed source says “this bean with light blue speckled seeds is from Clay County, Kentucky. Often grown as a bush bean, it does have short runners and does best with some support.”

Hidatsa Red

Kidney-type Bush Bean

A great way to grow your own kidney-type beans, this variety was grown by the Hidatsa Indians of present-day North Dakota. We intentionally sought out a seed source located in North Dakota to offer you Hidatsa beans that are as authentic as possible. Our seed source says they are “plump, dark red, dry beans ... one of the most productive dry beans available for prodigious harvests and long-term storage of high quality, high protein staples. This bean was introduced by Oscar H. Will & Co. in Bismarck, ND as part of his 1915 Pioneer Indian Collection. Semi-vining, productive bush plant will climb to three feet if given support. You'll be gifted with plump red beans, very similar to the kidney bean for use in chili, refried beans, and humus.” Enjoy!
